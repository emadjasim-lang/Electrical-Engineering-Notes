1. What is Power Factor?

Power factor is the ratio between real power and apparent power in an AC circuit. Real power performs useful work, such as producing heat, light, or mechanical output. Apparent power is the total power supplied by the source, including the part exchanged with magnetic and electric fields.

Power Factor = cos(φ) = P / S
S = √(P² + Q²)
P = real power in kW, Q = reactive power in kVAr, S = apparent power in kVA

A power factor close to 1.0 is usually desirable because the supply current is used more effectively. A low lagging power factor is common in circuits with induction motors, transformers, fluorescent lighting ballasts, and other inductive equipment.

In this website, lagging reactive power is shown as positive kVAr. Capacitor correction reduces the net reactive power and can improve the power factor.

2. Why is Power Factor Important?

Technical importance

Low power factor increases current for the same useful output. This can increase cable losses, transformer loading, voltage drop, and heating.

Economic importance

Many industrial users may face penalties or higher demand charges when reactive power demand is excessive.

System capacity

Improving power factor releases spare capacity in cables, switchgear, generators, and transformers without necessarily changing the real load.

Advantages of power factor improvement

  • Lower current for the same real power demand.
  • Reduced I²R losses in cables and transformers.
  • Improved voltage regulation.
  • Better utilisation of supply equipment.
  • Possible reduction in electricity charges for larger consumers.

Possible disadvantages or risks

  • Over-correction may produce a leading power factor, which can cause voltage rise or operational problems.
  • Capacitor banks may interact with harmonics and create resonance if not designed carefully.
  • Switching capacitors can create transient effects.
  • Maintenance and protection requirements increase when correction equipment is added.

3. Common Ways to Improve Power Factor

Method How it works Typical use Key caution
Capacitor bank Supplies capacitive reactive power to offset inductive kVAr. Commercial and industrial LV boards. Avoid over-correction and harmonic resonance.
Synchronous condenser A synchronous machine operated to absorb or supply reactive power. Large networks and substations. Higher cost and maintenance.
Variable speed drive Controls motor operation and can improve input current quality depending on design. Motor-driven systems. Harmonics must be considered.
Load management Reduces unnecessary inductive loading and avoids oversized motors running lightly loaded. Workshops, plant rooms, and production lines. Requires operational discipline.

7. Student Activities and Questions

Activity A: Simple circuit

Set P to 60 kW and QL to 55 kVAr. Adjust the capacitor until the power factor is close to 0.95 lagging. Record the required kVAr.

Students should reduce the net reactive power, not the real power. The capacitor supplies capacitive kVAr, so the net Q becomes smaller. A PF around 0.95 normally requires a moderate remaining lagging Q, not zero Q.

Activity B: Complicated circuit

Increase the induction motor loading to 90%. Then increase the capacitor bank and observe the cable loss index. Explain why the loss index changes.

Higher motor loading increases real power and reactive power. Low power factor causes more current for the same useful output. When the capacitor bank reduces the net reactive power, the apparent power and current reduce, so the I²R loss index also reduces.

Activity C: Critical thinking

Why should an engineer avoid correcting the power factor far beyond unity?

Excessive correction can create a leading power factor. This may cause voltage rise, unnecessary switching, poor compatibility with some equipment, and possible resonance with network inductance and harmonic currents.
